IRE 2026 · Hands-On Session

Why you should care about sports betting, even if you don't cover sports

During the 2026 NCAA tournament, we identified more than 21,000 opportunities to risk money on individual college athletes — most of them beyond the reach of state sports-betting law. This resource page walks through what a prop bet is and isn't, why prop betting data is hard to get, how to get it and what it shows. Follow along with the code in the public repo to explore sample data yourself, and use this code to build your own scrapers at home.

Cat Murphy · Shirley Povich Center for Sports Journalism & Howard Center for Investigative Journalism, University of Maryland

The problem

Prop bets on college athletes: an underreported beat with a data problem

A player prop is a wager on one athlete's individual stat line rather than on who wins. "Cameron Boozer over 22.5 points" is a prop. The bet is attached to a specific person, not a team or an outcome.

For professional athletes, this isn't new. The volume of props on NFL and NBA players, the movement of lines, the relationship between betting and player safety — reporters have been covering that for years. For college athletes, though, the equivalent data barely exists.

That gap matters because there are signs the same dynamics play out at the college level. Nearly 10% of Division I student-athletes who responded to an NCAA survey reported experiencing abuse related to betting losses — among men's basketball players, the figure was closer to half.

The NCAA has pushed for bans on college player props. Most states with legal sports betting restrict regulated sportsbooks from offering them. But how many opportunities exist to risk money on individual college athletes, and how many of those are actually offered by sportsbooks?

Related: March Madness is over. Abuse from bettors likely isn't — CNS Maryland / Povich Center & Howard Center, April 2026

Props per round

How prop volume grew as the tournament progressed

The Povich Center and Howard Center identified about 900 opportunities to risk money on individual college athletes during the First Four round of the 2026 men's college basketball tournament — an average of 224 per game.

The same platforms offered nearly 9,000 of these opportunities in the first round of the NCAA tournament, or about 278 props per game.

That figure climbed to an average of 306 per game during the second round.

By the Sweet 16, platforms were offering an average of 390 prop-like opportunities to risk money on individual college athletes per game.

The Elite Eight featured an average of 432 of these opportunities per game.

And in the Final Four, the number of available opportunities to risk money on individual college athletes surpassed 500 per game.

Platforms then closed out the tournament by offering a combined 478 player props on the national championship game between Michigan and UConn.

How we got there

Methodology

There is no public database of college player prop lines. Sportsbooks and daily fantasy platforms post them in real time and pull them at tip-off. No one compiles them. No regulator publishes them. If you want to know how many props were offered on a given player during March Madness, you have to scrape them yourself while they're still live.

The full project pulled lines from several sources. The public repo focuses on one provider, RotoWire — both its public and paid feeds — plus the ESPN schedule for game context. The same pipeline folds in any other source unchanged. The state eligibility analysis and the harassment reporting use additional inputs covered in METHODOLOGY.md. This section walks the loading and cleaning.

01

Scraping the sources

Everything in scrapers/ is a Python script driving Selenium with headless Chrome. Selenium because these are heavy JavaScript pages — lines load in after the page does, cookie banners have to be dismissed, content only appears after scrolling. A plain requests grab gets you almost nothing.

What's in the public repo:

  • espn_schedule_scraper.py — tournament schedule, matchups, seeds, regions, tip times, point spreads, over/unders. The backbone everything else hangs off of.
  • rotowire_public_data_scraper.py — RotoWire's public props page, which aggregates lines across several sportsbooks.
  • rotowire_subscription_data_downloader.py — the paid feed. The only source that reaches DFS apps like PrizePicks, Underdog, Sleeper and Pick6 and the only scraper that logs in. Reads credentials from a .env file that's never committed.

I don't know how to build a scraperYou don't need to be an engineer — I'm not. AI coding assistants (Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, Cursor) are good at this. Describe the site and the fields you want, paste in a sample of the page's HTML, and ask for a Python + Selenium script. Run it, paste any errors back, and keep iterating until it works — then spot-check the output against the live page. Start small: one page, a few fields, then build up.

02

Scraping on a schedule

I scraped several times a day because of how these markets behave. Books post props as they create them, on their own schedule and pull them when a game tips off — sometimes earlier. A prop I didn't catch before it came down was gone. Doing that by hand for three weeks wasn't realistic, so I automated it with GitHub Actions: scheduled workflows that spin up a fresh machine, run a scraper and commit the new data file back into the repo with a timestamp.

What's GitHub Actions?GitHub Actions runs your code on GitHub's servers — free for public repos, with no server to maintain. To schedule a scraper, add a workflow file under .github/workflows/ with a cron schedule, then steps that install Python, Chrome and your dependencies, run the scraper, and commit the new data file back to the repo. Each run spins up a fresh machine, and a manual "Run workflow" button lets you trigger it any time.

03

Standardizing the messy fields

This is mostly a cleaning project. ESPN's spread data calls UConn CONN. RotoWire calls them Connecticut. The bracket says UConn. One book lists a market as Reb+Ast, another as Rebounds + Assists. A player shows up as A.J. Dybantsa in one place and AJ Dybantsa in another. Until all of that agrees, nothing lines up and nothing joins.

So before combining anything, I run every source through the same cleanup, standardizing the handful of fields that have to match: team names, player names and the bet type (or "market" — points, rebounds, points + assists, and so on). I also tag each operator by type — a regulated sportsbook or a daily-fantasy app — since that split turns out to be central to the reporting. The demo notebook shows exactly how; the point here is that the same player, team or bet ends up written one way everywhere, so it can line up across sources.

04

Building the join key

The goal is a single tidy table where one row is one prop line from one operator. To get there, every prop has to be tied to a specific game — but sources don't agree on how to name a matchup: home/away order flips, abbreviations differ. So I build one consistent key for each game and join every source on it. Once a prop knows its game, it picks up that game's seeds, region, round and spread from the ESPN schedule.

RotoWire already tells me each player's team and opponent, so its rows land in a game on their own. But the moment you add a source that gives only a name and a bet with no team — DraftKings and BettingPros both do — you need a roster to look the player up first. A game is keyed on its two teams, so a name with no team can't be placed without it.

05

Merging into one table

With everything standardized and keyed to a game, the feeds stack into one table. The catch: the same bet often shows up more than once — across both feeds, or across the dozens of times a day I scraped. So every bet gets a prop_id: a unique fingerprint built from the player, book, market, teams and date. Identical bets share an ID, so I can collapse them into a single row — while keeping a count of how many times it turned up and which sources saw it, so no line loses its paper trail.

06

Join with other data

That clean, keyed table is a starting point, not the finish line. Because every prop carries its game, its teams and a player name, you can join it to almost anything else to ask new questions.

Demo

Try it yourself

Scraping is the collection step — that's covered up above. This is the other half: what to do with the prop data once you've got it. The whole loading-and-cleaning pipeline runs on the one-game sample that ships with the repo — no scraping, no credentials, no network — so you can turn a pile of mismatched files into one analyzable table in a couple of clicks. Everything is in github.com/catelizabethmurphy/ire26. It comes two ways — an R notebook and a Python notebook, same steps and same result — so use whichever you're comfortable in.

01

Download the code

No command line needed. On the repo page, click the green Code button, choose Download ZIP, and unzip it. (If you use it, Open with GitHub Desktop works too.)

02

Open it and run it

This is the sample code, in whichever language you prefer:

  • R — open sample_analysis/sample_analysis.Rmd in RStudio and click Knit. If a yellow bar offers to install missing packages, click Install.
  • Python — open sample_analysis/sample_analysis.ipynb in Jupyter and choose Run All.

Either way it reads the raw RotoWire and ESPN files and walks the standardization and merge end to end, building the one-row-per-prop table. No credentials needed.

Nothing installed? Run it in your browser — no setup, no download:

  • Python, one click: open the notebook in Google Colab, then Runtime → Run all.
  • R: open Posit Cloud (free) → New Project from Git Repository with the repo link → open the .Rmd → Knit.

Caveats & disclosure

Data notes

01

A note on AI assistance

I used AI coding tools to help build and debug the Python scrapers — wiring up the headless browser, dismissing cookie banners, handling lazy-loaded content and writing the site-specific parsers each source needed — and to help restructure the project repo into the demo you're looking at now. That means you can too. But the reporting logic, the standardization rules, the analysis and every editorial judgment are mine and I spot-checked the scraped output by hand against the live sites.

02

What the data misses

This is not every prop that was offered. It's a floor and it undercounts for two reasons.

First, takedowns. Books pull props once a game starts, so anything posted and removed between two of my scrapes — or only put up after my last run before tip-off — never got captured. The scraping window and the betting window didn't line up perfectly and the gap is lost props.

Second, uneven coverage across sites. The public version here is RotoWire-only: the public feed is sportsbook-only, while the paid feed is what brings in the DFS apps that drove much of the action on college players. But RotoWire relays only the lines it chooses to, so even with both feeds the DFS apps are undercounted — scraping them directly would surface more. The full project added more sources to chip away at this, but never closed the gap completely.

Treat the counts as a minimum, not a complete tally.

Where to take this

How to go further

The cleaning step is deliberately a starting point. The game key and prop_id are built so you can keep joining things onto the table and the standardization functions take any new source. Here's where I'd point a reporter who wanted to push past this one-game, RotoWire-only sample.

01

Add more betting sources

RotoWire is one provider. The full project also scraped DraftKings (two ways — a top-props list and a game-by-game walk) and BettingPros (opening lines and current odds across many books at once). Each is just another scraper feeding the same standardization functions, then stacked onto the pile and deduped on prop_id, so a line seen on three sites collapses to one row with number_times_identified = 3.

The bigger prize is the DFS apps themselves — PrizePicks, Underdog, Sleeper, Pick6, Fliff. RotoWire's paid feed relays some of their lines, but only the ones it chooses to carry and without each app's full board or pricing. Scraping the apps directly is the single biggest way to close the coverage gap, because they're where most of the action on college players lives — and, as the eligibility strand shows, where it reaches the most and youngest people.

02

Add rosters to place name-only bets

RotoWire hands you each player's team, so its rows drop into a game on their own. But the moment you add a source that gives only a name and a bet with no team — DraftKings and BettingPros both do — you need a roster as a bridge. A game is keyed on its pair of teams, so a name with no team can't be placed without first looking the name up: roster → team → game. The full project scrapes each school's official roster page for exactly this, which doubles as a cross-check that every propped player is really on a tournament roster. (Common names still collide, so the join needs spot-checking.)

03

Add box scores to grade the bets

The clean table tells you a bet existed; it doesn't tell you how it resolved. ESPN's public game-summary API returns every player's full stat line by game ID. Join that on the game and player keys and you can grade every prop over or under — the most obvious next join, since this cleaning step carries only player_minutes.

04

Social handles and the harassment reporting

The roster pages do double duty. Alongside player names, schools link each player's social accounts — Instagram, X, TikTok, Facebook — right on the roster profile, so these are real, school-listed handles rather than anything I had to guess at. Those handles are the first link in the harassment side of this reporting: the chain runs handle → the player's posts → the comments on them, which is how a specific abusive comment gets tied back to the specific (often teenage) athlete it targeted. The comment data isn't in this repo, but the handle collection is where that thread starts. If you take this on, be deliberate about commenter and victim data.

05

Patterns worth chasing

A few questions the data is built to answer that I noticed while reporting but haven't fully pinned down — good places to start if you pick this up:

  • Does March Madness look different from the regular season? The tournament is a compressed, high-attention window. Pointing the same scrapers at regular-season college games would show whether the sheer volume — and the DFS-vs-sportsbook split — hold up once the spotlight is off.
  • Does tip time change how many props a game draws? Every scrape is timestamped and the schedule carries each game's tip time, so you can line the two up. My experience during the regular season was that games get more betting attention when they are the only game on — random 10 p.m. West Coast games would have a bunch of props available, whereas a similar mid-major game at 4 p.m. on a Tuesday wouldn't.
  • How did a line move before tip? RotoWire has a separate dataset that identifies line movements, if you're into that sort of thing.
  • How much did operators disagree? With one row per operator, compare books and apps on the same player-stat line and flag where they diverge most.

Looking back

Lessons learned: what I'd do differently

A few things I'd change if I ran this again — most of them about reach, not method.

  • Dig deeper into DFS sites that weren't captured by this analysis. I started this work long before March Madness, spending months on what to scrape, how to clean it, how to merge it, how to contextualize it. Even so, there are sites I learned about just a little too late to incorporate.
  • Look more at prediction markets. Prediction markets are regulated differently from both DFS and sports betting and while they didn't offer the same volume of trading opportunities on individual college players, college players would occasionally pop up on those platforms as well.
  • Experiment more with women's college props. These were far harder to track — they rarely appeared on regulated sportsbooks and only sporadically on more obscure DFS sites. I still wish I had captured more data on this.
  • Analyze college football data. This was a timing thing; the project ran on a spring semester. I've heard anecdotally that college football props are as ridiculous as the college basketball ones, if not more so.
